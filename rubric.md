# Rubric — how we grade

> How the lens judges any item. This is the *stable* half of the lens (your taste and bar); `priorities.md` is the *moving* half (what the business cares about right now). The lens reads both. Tune the tiers below to your instinct.

## Impact tiers

**MUST-KNOW** — you'd be caught flat-footed in a C-suite conversation without it. Time-sensitive, widely discussed *right now*, and it directly shapes what we should be saying this week. These lead the brief.

**HIGH-IMPACT** — not urgent, but materially useful: a sharp framing, a genuine shift, or a proof point you'd want in the arsenal for a POV or report. Logged to the ledger.

**SIGNAL** — worth logging for pattern-tracking, not worth foregrounding today. One recurrence is noise; the same signal three weeks running is a trend the year-end report should catch. Ledger only.

**NOISE** — drop silently. No line, no apology.

## Framing instincts

When assessing and writing the why-us line:

1. **Read for the mechanic, not the headline.** What's the underlying cultural or behavioural shift, not just the news event?
2. **Connect to our throughlines.** An item is more valuable if it extends, complicates, or proves an argument we already own (see `priorities.md`).
3. **Prefer what we could *say* over what merely happened.** The output is a comms opportunity, not a summary. If there's nothing to say, it's probably SIGNAL at best.
4. **Whitespace beats consensus.** An angle peers are all making is less valuable than one they're missing.
5. **Verify before elevating.** Anything tiered MUST-KNOW must be anchored to a real, checked source — never a vibe.

## Default behaviour before `priorities.md` is filled

Grade on prominence + freshness + relevance to social/brand-building thought leadership, and write a generic why-us. Flag in the brief that scoring is running un-tuned until priorities are set.
